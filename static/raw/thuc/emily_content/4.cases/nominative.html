Nominative

The word "nominative" originally comes from the Greek word ὄνομα, meaning "name". Thus, the nominative case is used to indicate the subject of a sentence or the complement of the verb.

Useful tip! It helps to identify the main verb of a sentence first. Once you have found the verb, it becomes easier to understand the function of the other words in the sentence.

Nominative as the subject

Examples:

Thucydides describes the war.

ὁ Θουκυδίδης ἐξηγέται τόν πόλεμον.

The day is long.

ἡ ἡμέρα εστίν μακρά.

Nominative as the complement

Examples:

Thucydides is a historian.

ὁ Θουκυδίδης εστίν συγγραφεύς.

Sicily is an island.

Σικελίαεστίν νῆσος.

The complement, which is also a noun in the nominative, always follows the verb to be. Now you can see why spotting the verb first can help you understand the function of the other words in the sentence!

Useful tip! The Greek word order might surprise you. You will find that the word order doesn't follow particular conventions and the verb may often be at the end of a sentence!
