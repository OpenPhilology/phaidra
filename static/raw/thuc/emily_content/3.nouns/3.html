Case

The last thing to remember about Greek nouns is that their ending changes according to the case they are in. Cases indicate the function of word in a sentence.

Take the example: Thucydides describes the war.

Thucydides is the subject or nominative of the verb describes, while the war is the object or accusative of the sentence.

In Greek there are 5 main cases:
  • Nominative
  • Accusative
  • Genitive
  • Dative
  • Vocative

The ending of a noun will always indicate which case it is in. Both a noun and its corresponding definite article will vary according to case. Though you haven't learnt what each case represents, it would be useful for you to first familiarize with the Greek articles, in order to make the next lesson - "Introduction to cases" - easier to understand.

In this lesson you have learnt that:

  • Greek nouns have a gender (feminine, masculine or neuter)
  • Greek nouns have a number singular or plural)
  • Greek nouns have a case (nominative, accusative, genitive, dative or vocative)
  • When Greek nouns are preceded by a definite article, they will take the same gender, number and case as the article itself
