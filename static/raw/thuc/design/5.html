What does Nominative mean?

The word "nominative" originally comes from the Greek word ὄνομα, meaning "name". Thus the nominative case is used to indicate the subject of a sentence or the predicate noun or adjective.

Subjects and Predicates in English

There are three situations where Greek will require a word to be in the nominative case, each in bold text.

  1. Jane picked a flower.
    Here, "Jane" is the subject.
  2. Jane is a girl.
    Here, "Jane" is still the subject, but "girl" is now a predicate noun because, as a noun, it refers to ("predicates on") the subject, Jane, and is connected by "is", a linking verb .
  3. Jane is strong.
    As before, "Jane" remains the subject, but "strong" is now a predicate adjective because, as an adjective, it predicates on the subject, Jane, and is connected by a linking verb.

If these examples were written in Greek, Jane, girl, and strong would all be in the nominative case.
