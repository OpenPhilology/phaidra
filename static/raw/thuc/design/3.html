More on Case

Greek has a total of five grammatical cases, each of which has its own job in completing the meaning of a sentence.

Some of the grammatical roles that a case can have include:

Indicating the Subject

The συμμαχία (alliance) of the Delian League was strong.

A οἰκία (house) sat high upon the hill.

This would a job for the nominative case.

Indicating the Direct Object

The general broke the συμμαχίαν (alliance).

The storm destroyed that οἰκίαν (house) on the hill.

This would a job for the accusative case.

Indicating the Indirect Object

The general presented a new treaty συμμαχίᾳ (to the alliance).

They made a new roof οἰκίᾳ (for the house).

This would a job for the dative case.

The ending of a noun will always indicate which case it is in.

To change a noun from one case into an other, you must use the stem of the noun and append endings based on the declension to which it belongs.

Case Examples
1. Nominative Case Used as the subject of the sentence
The soldiers ran into battle.
2. Genitive Case a. Expressing posession (often demonstrated in English with of or from)
That is the shield of a soldier.
b. Indicating motion away, or separation (often demonstrated in English by away from or out of)
The enemies fled from the battle.
3. Dative Case a. Used as the indirect object of a verb (often demonstrated in English by to or for)
The leader gave encouragement to the soldiers.
b. Shows instrumentality (often demonstrated in English by by and with)
The order was written with a pen.
4. Accusative Case Used for direct objects of a verb.
The sword struck the shield.
5. Vocative Case Used to show that a noun is being addressed directly.
O brother, where art though?
